Founder

Name: Genghis Khan (born Temüjin)

Founded: 1206, upon unification of the Mongol tribes

Accomplishments: United the Mongol tribes, launched conquests across Eurasia, and established the largest contiguous land empire in history. Introduced the Yam communication system and promoted religious tolerance.

Cause of Death: Uncertain—possibly battle injuries or a fall from his horse

Location: Near the Liupan Mountains, Western Xia territory

Achievements

  • Unified Mongol tribes under a single banner
  • Conquered vast territories from China to Eastern Europe
  • Established the largest contiguous land empire in history
  • Created the Yam system for rapid communication
  • Promoted religious tolerance and meritocracy

Defeats

  • Failed to conquer Japan due to typhoons (Kamikaze)
  • Defeated at the Battle of Ain Jalut (1260) by the Mamluks
  • Internal fragmentation after Kublai Khan's reign

Timeline

  • 1206: Temüjin declared Genghis Khan, ruler of all Mongols
  • 1215: Capture of Zhongdu (Beijing)
  • 1227: Death of Genghis Khan
  • 1241: Mongol invasion of Europe reaches Hungary and Poland
  • 1260: Defeat at Ain Jalut halts western expansion
  • 1271: Kublai Khan founds the Yuan Dynasty in China
  • 1294: Death of Kublai Khan; empire begins to fragment

Fortified Holdings

Held: Zhongdu – Captured in 1215, became a key administrative center.

Held: Samarkand – Seized during Central Asian campaigns.

Held: Karakorum – Capital of the Mongol Empire under Ögedei.

Contested: Baghdad – Sacked in 1258, ending the Abbasid Caliphate.

Contested: Kiev – Devastated in 1240 during western expansion.

Contested: Xiangyang – Besieged during Yuan conquest of Song China.

Lost: Ain Jalut – Site of Mongol defeat by Mamluks in 1260.

Lost: Delhi – Briefly raided but not held long-term.

Troop Units

  • Mongol Horse Archers — Fast, deadly, and tactically dominant
  • Heavy Cavalry — Armored shock troops used in decisive charges
  • Siege Engineers — Experts in Chinese and Persian siege technology
  • Chinese Engineers — Experts in siegecraft, fortification design, and logistics. Crucial in campaigns against walled cities in China and Central Asia.
  • Persian Artillery Specialists — Provided advanced siege weapons like mangonels and trebuchets. Key to breaching fortified cities in Persia and the Levant.
  • Turkic Cavalry — Light and medium cavalry from Central Asia. Used for flanking, pursuit, and reinforcing Mongol horse archer tactics.
  • Korean Naval Units — Contributed ships and sailors during Mongol invasions of Japan and coastal operations in East Asia.
  • Chinese Infantry — Provided massed foot soldiers for garrison duty, urban combat, and support roles in southern campaigns.
  • Shamanic Units — Spiritual advisors and ritual specialists embedded with Mongol forces. Used for morale, divination, and ceremonial functions.
